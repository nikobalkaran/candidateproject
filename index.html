Here's a Look at Four of Senator Kirsten Gillibrand's Priority Issues

By Niko Balkaran

At a Glance

  • Senator Kirsten Gillibrand is part of the Democratic Party in New York City.
  • Gillibrand is a candidate for both the Democratic and Working Families Party in the upcoming general election for the U.S. Senate in New York on November 5.
  • Gillibrand is the incumbent candidate and is running against Mike Sapraicone of the Republican and Conservative Party and Diane Sare of the LaRouche Party.
  • Gillibrand is on several committees. She is the chair of the Senate Armed Services Subcommittee on Emerging Threats and Capabilities. She is also on the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence, Senate Agriculture Committee, and Senate Aging Committee.

So before the polls are open, New Yorkers may want to consider her stance on four key issues before heading to the polls.

Immigration

Senator Gillibrand is an avid supporter of immigration reform and fairness. She was one of several politicians who introduced the Fairness to Freedom Act of 2023, which would allow anyone facing deportation to retain counsel.

Education

One of Senator Gillibrand's priorities is education for all ages. Some key areas that she supports are universal pre-K, affordable college, and free meals for all schoolchildren. Recently, Gillibrand introduced the Universal School Meals Program Act, which aims to give all schoolchildren free breakfast, lunch, and dinner.

Environment

As part of the Senate, Gillibrand helped to pass America’s biggest climate bill in 2022. In September, along with Senate Majority Leader Charles Schumer and Congressman Tim Kennedy, she announced $1.7 million in federal funding to restore Ralph C. Wilson Jr. Centennial Park in Buffalo and some parts of the Niagara River Shoreline.

Health Care

Senator Gillibrand believes that access to not only affordable but also “high-quality” health care "should be a basic human right.” Subsidies that make health insurance more affordable for Americans can stop by the end of 2025 if Congress does not extend them. As a result, Gillibrand announced the Health Care Affordability Act earlier this month so that New Yorkers can still have access to health insurance.
